Licenciatura Thesis: Formalization

This project involves the formal verification of my Licenciatura Thesis in Mathematics (equivalent to a combined B.S. + M.S.), titled "Dualities for bounded distributive lattices with normal operators". The goal is to fully formalize the construction of Priestley Spaces and their duality with Distributive Lattices using the Lean 4 theorem prover.

> ./theorems --list-verified

  • Priestley Duality Formal construction of the category of Priestley Spaces and its equivalence to Distributive Lattices.
  • Canonical Extensions Proof of existence of canonical extensions for bounded distributive lattices with monotone operators.

This work is part of a larger effort to digitize 20th-century order theory results, making them accessible to AI-driven proof search algorithms.

> ./thesis --abstract

This thesis develops a representation theory for bounded distributive lattices equipped with normal unary operators. Two complementary approaches are pursued.

The first establishes a Priestley-type duality for these structures, generalizing classical Priestley duality. For each pair of operator types (i, j) — encoding whether the operator preserves or reverses joins and meets — we construct dual categories of topological-relational spaces and prove an equivalence of categories between the algebraic and topological settings. A symmetry phenomenon between dual operator types is identified and formalized.

The second approach studies the canonical extension of bounded distributive lattices with normal unary operators, characterizing the behavior of operators on open and closed elements and deriving a relational representation for each operator type.

The two approaches are shown to be consistent: the relational representations obtained via duality and via canonical extension coincide. As an application, Ockham algebras are recovered as subcategories of the framework, and equational properties of operators — such as preservation of unions, idempotence, and commutativity — are shown to correspond to relational properties of the dual spaces, namely closure, reflexivity, and transitivity.
